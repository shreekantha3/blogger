Understanding RTI Act 2005: Your Right to Know

The Right to Information Act 2005 is a landmark legislation that empowers citizens to seek information from public authorities. It was enacted by Parliament on October 12, 2005, and came into force on October 12, 2005, with some provisions effective from June 15, 2005.

The Act is based on the principle that citizens have the right to know about government functioning and holds public officials accountable. It replaced the earlier Freedom of Information Act 2002 with a stronger, more enforceable framework.

Study Notes: RTI Act Essentials

Key Definitions

Public Authority: Any authority, body, or institution owned, controlled, or financed by government funds (Section 2(1)).

Information: Includes records, documents, circulars, orders, and electronic formats (Section 2(f)).

Right to Information: Right to see any information, question officials, and inspect work, documents, or records (Section 3).

Institutional Mechanisms

  • Public Information Officer (PIO): First point of contact for RTI applications
  • First Appellate Authority (FAA): Reviews PIO decisions within department
  • Central Information Commission (CIC): States have their own ICs
  • Information Commissioner: Appointed by President on recommendation of selection committee

Important Amendments

The 2005 Act was amended in 2019, introducing transparency in political parties' funding, and in 2012 with provisions for suo moto disclosure by public authorities.

FAQ

Q: What is the time limit for providing information?

A: 30 days normally, 48 hours for life/threat related information, 45 days for third-party information.

Q: Can RTI be filed online?

A: Yes, through RTI Online Portal (rtionline.gov.in) for central government and respective state portals.
