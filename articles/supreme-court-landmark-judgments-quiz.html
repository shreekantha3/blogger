Understanding Landmark Judgments: The Living Constitution

Supreme Court judgments breathe life into constitutional provisions, interpreting text in contemporary context. From the basic structure doctrine in Kesavananda to privacy rights in Puttaswamy, these cases have shaped modern India.

For competitive exam aspirants, understanding the key judgments and their implications is essential. Each case represents a turning point in constitutional jurisprudence - Golaknath vs Parliament's amending power, Maneka Gandhi expanding Article 21, and Navtej Singh Johar decriminalizing Section 377.

This quiz tests your knowledge of the most frequently asked cases in General Studies papers. You'll learn not just what each case decided, but why it mattered for our constitutional democracy.

Study Notes: Landmark Cases

Golaknath v. State of Punjab (1967)

Held that Parliament cannot amend fundamental rights. Led to 24th Amendment restoring Parliament's amending power. Significant for establishing judicial review of constitutional amendments.

Kesavananda Bharati (1973)

"Basic structure" doctrine: Parliament can amend any part except the basic structure. Identified supremacy, republican government, secular character, separation of powers, federal character, unity/integrity, welfare state, judicial review as basic elements.

Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)

Expanded "procedure established by law" to require fairness, reasonableness, and justness. Overturned the narrow interpretation from A.K. Gopalan. Made Article 21 meaningful.

Minerva Mills (1980)

Established that neither fundamental rights nor directive principles can dominate. Both are complementary. Struck down clauses of 42nd Amendment that gave exclusive primacy to DPSP.

S.R. Bommai (1994)

Federal structure is part of basic structure. Floor test is legitimate method. Parliamentary ratification mandatory within 2 months. Judicial review of political decisions.

Justice K.S. Puttaswamy (2017)

Right to privacy is fundamental. Requires comprehensive privacy legislation. Nine-judge bench unanimously declared privacy as fundamental right.

FAQ

Q: What is the basic structure doctrine?

A: Parliament can amend Constitution but cannot alter its basic structure (Kesavananda case).

Q: Which case decriminalized homosexuality?

A: Navtej Singh Johar (2018) decriminalized consensual homosexual acts.
